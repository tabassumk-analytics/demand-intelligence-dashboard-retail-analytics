Executive insights

Sales concentration is materially higher at the store level than at the item level. The top 4 stores contribute 47.73% of total sales, while the top 10 items contribute 31.42%, indicating that performance risk is more concentrated across store locations than across products.

Demand follows a clear calendar pattern. Sales peak in July and reach their strongest weekday level on Sunday, which signals repeatable seasonality that can be used for replenishment timing, staffing, and promotional planning.

Store stability is more mixed than item stability. The store portfolio includes both stable core contributors and higher-variability stores, while the item portfolio is more polarized between dependable high contributors and volatile lower contributors.

Taken together, the business appears to rely on a relatively small group of core stores to carry a disproportionate share of demand, while product performance is more distributed but still structured around a stable core item set.

---
Business recommendations

Prioritize inventory and capacity planning around the July peak and Sunday demand concentration. These periods should receive tighter service-level monitoring, replenishment focus, and operational readiness planning.

Create a watchlist for high-contribution stores with elevated variability. These stores matter disproportionately to topline performance, so volatility in their demand should trigger earlier forecasting review and exception handling.

Protect the core stable contributor base. High-contribution, low-variability stores and items should be treated as operational anchors with stronger in-stock discipline and tighter execution controls.

Review the low-contribution, high-variability tail for simplification opportunities. Items or store-product combinations that add variability without meaningful sales contribution may be candidates for assortment cleanup, localized strategy, or lower planning intensity.
